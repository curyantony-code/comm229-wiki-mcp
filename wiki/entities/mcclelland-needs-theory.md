# McClelland's Needs Theory of Motivation

## What it is

McClelland's Needs Theory is a **content (needs) theory** of motivation that identifies three learned needs driving human behaviour. Unlike Maslow's hierarchy, these needs are **non-hierarchical** — one or more may be active at any time depending on context and individual background. When the relevant need is satisfied, the person feels fulfilled; motivation is driven by whichever need is currently strongest.

## Components

| Need | Description | Signature behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| **Need for Achievement (nAch)** | Strong desire to perform challenging tasks well; to solve problems; to excel | Sets stretch goals; prefers moderate difficulty; seeks feedback on results |
| **Need for Affiliation (nAff)** | Strong desire to establish and maintain friendly, close relationships | Prefers cooperative environments; avoids conflict; values belonging |
| **Need for Power (nPow)** | Strong desire to influence others, make an impact, be in control | Seeks leadership roles; enjoys directing others; motivated by status |

## Applications in COMM 229

- **Week 9 (Motivation & Rewards):** Listed alongside SDT, Expectancy, Equity, and Goal-Setting as part of the motivation theories canon
- **Wize Midterm 1 (Ch5):** Positioned as the primary *needs/content* theory contrasted with process theories

## Common Misconceptions

- **Not a hierarchy.** Unlike Maslow, McClelland's three needs do not follow a ranked sequence. All three can coexist; context determines which is most active.
- **Confused with Maslow's hierarchy or Herzberg's two-factor theory.** McClelland is the only non-hierarchical needs theory in the COMM 229 canon.
- **Achievement ≠ ambition in general.** nAch specifically predicts preference for *moderately challenging* tasks — too easy or too hard does not satisfy the need.
- **Power ≠ dominance.** Socialized power (nPow) in management is about *influencing and helping* the organization, not personal domination.
