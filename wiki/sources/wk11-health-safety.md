## Ensuring Health & Safety

### Historical Shift
**Early approach (1900s):** Workers accepted customary risks; "assumption of risk" was a legal expression; scars were "badges of honour."

**Safety in the Workplace Models:**
| Model | Description |
|---|---|
| **Careless Worker Model** | Early approach — accidents due to workers' carelessness |
| **Shared Responsibility Model** | Modern approach — cooperation of employer AND employees |

### Workplace Injuries Statistics
- Average of 2.5 employees die each day from workplace accidents (925/year in Canada)
- $8B annually in direct compensation; >$19B including indirect expenses
- 1 in 7 young workers is injured on the job
- 25% of all workplace injuries involve workers aged 15–29
- Among injured workers under 25: 50% injured in first 6 months; 20% of injuries/fatalities in first month

### Health Hazards
- **Physical agents:** Noise, extreme temperature, etc.
- **Biological agents:** Viruses, natural organisms
- **Ergonomic related:** Repetitive strain, poor workstation design

## Laws About Health & Safety

### Three Fundamental Employee Rights
1. **The right to know** about hazards in the workplace
2. **The right to participate** in correcting hazards
3. **The right to refuse** dangerous work

### Key Legislation
- **Workplace Health and Safety Committee:** Deals with complaints, investigations, inspections (at least annually)
- **Hazardous Products Act:** Protects consumers by regulating sale of dangerous products
- **Transportation of Dangerous Goods Act**
- **Canadian Centre for Occupational Health and Safety Act**
- **WHMIS (Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System):** Requires employer training to recognize and understand hazards; includes Hazardous Products Act

> **Exam tip:** The most effective way to communicate safety rules is through a **new employee orientation program** (not just bulletin boards or manuals).

## Who's Responsible?

**Legal requirements are the *minimum*. Top Canadian employers go further.**

**Responsibility is shifting from employers to employees** (joint committees).

**Hierarchy of responsibility:**
- Top Management: set policies, lead by example
- Supervisors: day-to-day enforcement
- Employees: comply, report hazards

**Implications for HRM:**
- Ensure consistent enforcement
- Best defence to an OHS offence is **due diligence**
- Health and safety audit
- Safety climate
- Downsizing and safety (cuts can increase risk)

## Stress

### Definition
"The harmful physical and emotional responses that can happen when there is a conflict between job demands of the employee and the amount of control the employee has over meeting those demands."

### Causes of Stress
| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Working conditions** | Workload/pace, shift work, physical environment, isolation, travel |
| **Role** | Role conflict, role ambiguity, unrealistic demands, level of responsibility |
| **Career** | Under/over-promotion, job security, job satisfaction, pay |
| **Relationships at work** | Supervisors, co-workers, threat of violence or harassment, incivility |
| **Management practices** | Lack of support, autonomy, communication, resources |
| **Life events** | Work-life balance, family/personal issues, illness |

**Acute (infrequent) vs. Chronic (ongoing) stressors**

### Consequences of Stress
**Physical:** Cardiovascular disease, musculoskeletal disorders, stomach upsets
**Psychological:** Nervousness, chronic worry, defence mechanisms, depression, burnout
**Behavioural:** Problem solving, seeking support; OR ↓ performance, withdrawal, substance abuse

### Stress can be Helpful or Harmful
- At optimal stress level → peak performance
- Too little stress → boredom, low challenge, low performance
- Too much stress → burnout, withdrawal

**Burnout:** Mental, emotional, and sometimes physical exhaustion from substantial and prolonged stress.
**Presenteeism:** Attending work but inhibited from achieving optimal productivity due to ongoing health issues.

### Individual Differences in Stress Response
- Outlook and attitude
- Personality and coping style
- Work-life balance
- Support network (family, friends, co-workers)
- Health (exercise, etc.)

> **Instructor note:** Though individual differences matter, evidence shows certain working conditions are stressful to *most* people — don't over-individualize.

## Stress Management

**Curative:** Try to correct the *outcomes* of stress (counselling, exercise programs)
**Preventive:** Try to change the *causes* of stress (stress management training)
**Stress audit:** Identifies the causes of stress

### 5 Types of Stress Management Practices
1. **Remove the stressor** (eliminate the cause)
2. **Withdraw from the stressor** (take breaks, reassign)
3. **Change stress perceptions** (cognitive reappraisal)
4. **Control stress consequences** (relaxation, exercise)
5. **Receive social support** (peers, supervisors, EAP)

### HR Actions to Reduce Stress
Establish policy · Provide training · Adjust work schedules · Set compatible workload · Enable social interaction · Job design · Define roles clearly · Enable participation in decisions

## Other Contemporary Safety Issues

### Mental Health
- Mental health costs Canada ~$50B/year; ~$6B in lost productivity
- ~6.7 million Canadians have a mental health problem or illness
- ~500,000 Canadians absent any given week due to mental health
- ~30% of disability claims; ~70% of disability costs attributable to mental illness

### Fitness & Employee Wellness Programs
Improve employee health · Decrease health care costs · Improve satisfaction · Decrease absenteeism and turnover · Improve corporate image · Reduce disability claims

### Other Issues
- **Workplace violence and security**
- **Sick Building Syndrome (SBS)**
- **Ergonomics**

### Microaggressions
"Everyday verbal, nonverbal, and environmental slights, snubs, or insults, whether intentional or unintentional, which communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages to target persons based solely upon their marginalized group membership." (Dr. Sue)

**Micro-intervention Strategies:**
1. Make the "invisible" visible
2. Disarm the microaggression
3. Educate the perpetrator
4. Seek outside support/help
