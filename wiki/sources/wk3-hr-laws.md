# Week 3: HR Laws

## Learning Objectives
1. Determine which federal/provincial employment standards apply
2. List groups protected from discrimination under the Canadian Human Rights Act
3. Describe the purpose of equity legislation
4. Describe the purpose of privacy legislation
5. Define harassment and HR's role in addressing it
6. List types of HR policies organizations may adopt

## Key Content

### Why Employment Law?
Laws protect workers, reflect societal values, set standards for what can/cannot be done. Must be followed — ignorance is not an excuse. Necessary for managing a multicultural workforce.

### Which Laws Apply? — 3 Questions
1. **Employee or independent contractor?**
   - Employees → governed by employment standards (vacation, holidays, etc.)
   - Independent contractors → governed only by their contract
   - Determined by: (1) level of employer control, (2) ownership of tools, (3) worker's opportunity for profit/loss
   > **Exam tip:** Even if someone is called a "contractor," courts look at actual working conditions. Controlling schedule + company equipment + exclusivity = likely employee.

2. **Provincially or federally regulated?**
   - Federal: federal government, banks, airlines, rail/ship transportation (e.g., CIBC, Air Canada, Bell) — ~10% of workers
   - Provincial: most employees (~90%)

3. **Unionized or non-unionized?**
   - Unionized → covered by provincial labour relations acts (collective bargaining)
   - Non-unionized → covered by provincial employment standards acts

### Four Bodies of Canadian Employment Legislation
1. **Employment Standards Laws**
2. **Human Rights Laws**
3. **Privacy Laws**
4. **Equity Laws**

---

## Employment Standards
**Goal:** Specify minimum employer obligations (hours of work, minimum wages, overtime pay, vacation pay, statutory holidays).
- Federal level: *Canada Labour Code*
- Quebec level: *Act respecting Labour Standards* (CNESST enforces)
- Applies to all employers (unionized or not)

---

## Human Rights

### Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms
Fundamental rights: freedom of conscience/religion, thought/expression, peaceful assembly, association.

### Canadian Human Rights Act — Protected Grounds
Ethnicity · Race · Ethnic/national origin · Religion · Age · Sex · Sexual orientation · Marital status · Family status · Disability status · Pardoned offences

> **Exam tip:** Human rights legislation affects *nearly every HR function*, unlike employment standards which affect one activity at a time.

### Discrimination
- **Direct discrimination:** Treating someone adversely on protected grounds — illegal (unless BFOQ)
- **Indirect/Systemic discrimination:** A policy/practice not openly discriminatory but with discriminatory *effect* (e.g., minimum height requirements, seniority-based promotion favouring one group)
- **Reverse discrimination:** Preference given to a protected group member over an equally or better qualified non-member
- **BFOQ (Bona Fide Occupational Qualification):** Discrimination is permitted if the employer can establish necessity for business operations
- **Duty to accommodate:** Employer must adjust policies/practices to prevent discrimination — up to the point of *undue hardship*

### Bill 21 (Quebec — Loi sur la Laïcité)
Bars public servants in authority positions (judges, police, teachers, etc.) from wearing religious symbols. Quebec amended the Charter of Human Rights to recognize State laicity as fundamental. Controversial HR implications.

## Harassment

### Definition
Behaviour aimed at humiliating another person. May include unwanted physical or verbal conduct. Neglects the inherent dignity of each individual.

### Types
- **Sexual harassment:** Unsolicited or unwelcome sex/gender-based conduct with adverse employment consequences
- **Psychological harassment (5 criteria):** Vexatious behaviour; repetitive; verbal comments or gestures that are hostile and unwelcomed; affects dignity/integrity; creates a harmful work environment

### Key Rule
Employer retaliation against employees who file human rights charges is a **criminal act**. Enforcement: CHRC or provincial human rights bodies.

### Anti-Harassment Policy Elements
- Threats, intimidation, verbal abuse
- Unwelcome jokes about appearances
- Offensive pictures
- Sexually suggestive remarks
- Inappropriate physical conduct
- Exclusion or bullying

## Equity Laws

### Pay Equity
Equal pay for work of *equal value* — seeks to eliminate the gender wage gap. Difficult to determine "equal value" for dissimilar jobs.

### Employment Equity
A distinctly Canadian process for achieving equality in all aspects of employment for four designated groups: **Women, First Nations peoples, Visible minorities, People with disabilities.** Ensures equitable representation. *Mandatory* (unlike diversity management which is voluntary).

---

## Privacy Legislation
Governs collection, storage, and access to personal information about employees (race, religion, age, marital status, medical history, SIN, etc.).
- **Privacy Act:** Right to access and correct personal information the Government of Canada holds
- **PIPEDA:** Sets rules for how organizations collect, use, and disclose personal information in Canada

## Diversity, Equity & Inclusion (DEI)
See [[concepts/dei]] for full synthesis.

### Definitions
- **Diversity:** Recognizing the presence of differences
- **Equity:** Ensuring access to the same opportunities
- **Inclusion:** Welcoming and valuing all people

### Benefits of Diversity
Larger candidate pool · More creative decisions · Better customer understanding · More positive org image · Higher retention · Higher company performance · Higher stock prices

### Challenges of Diversity
- **Similarity-attraction phenomenon:** Tendency to be attracted to similar people (surface → deep level diversity assumptions)
- **Ethnocentrism:** Belief one's own culture is superior
- **Stereotyping:** Grouping people without considering individuality
- **Glass ceiling:** Invisible obstructions to career advancement of women and visible minorities
- **Old Boy's Network:** Informal relationships among male managers
- **Pet to Threat:** Phenomenon where Black women who were supported become perceived as threats

### Managing Diversity
Make leaders accountable · Build culture that embraces diversity · DEI training programs · Review HR practices · Be systematic: assess needs, set goals, monitor progress

### DEI Initiatives
- Inclusive internal systems (communication, hiring, reward structures)
- DEI training (awareness, skill-building, content vs. process)
- Alternate work arrangements
- Apprenticeships
- Support groups
- Communication standards
