# Wize Midterm 2 / Final Crash Course

**Source:** Wizedemy Inc. — third-party exam prep booklet for COMM 229 Midterm 2 / Final  
**Chapters covered:** Ch8/10/11 (Planning, Recruitment, Selection), Ch12 (Onboarding, Training & Development), Ch13 (Performance Management), Ch5/6 (Organizational Culture & Change)

---

## Learning Objectives

- Apply HR planning forecasting methods and distinguish demand vs. supply tools
- Differentiate internal vs. external recruitment advantages and disadvantages
- Apply selection criteria: reliability, validity, interview types, employment assessments
- Distinguish onboarding, training, and development; apply the ADDIE/ISD framework
- Describe Kirkpatrick's four evaluation levels and transfer of training
- Identify PM purposes, review methods (trait/behavioural/results/comparative), and failure reasons
- Define organizational culture dimensions, diagnose culture, describe merging strategies
- Apply change management approaches: Force Field Analysis, Action Research, Appreciative Inquiry, LGI

## Key Content

### Ch8/10/11 — HR Planning, Recruitment & Selection

**HR Planning:**
- **HR planning:** Process to ensure the organization has people with right competencies, effectively utilized
- **Forecasting demand:**
  - *Trend analysis:* Past numerical data → patterns → predict future demand
  - *Management (expert) forecasts:* Ask managers for predictions
- **Forecasting supply:**
  - *Staffing table:* Graphs showing current jobs + anticipated demand
  - *Markov analysis:* How employees move into, within, and out of the organization
  - *Skills inventory:* Education, experience, skills of current employees
- **Gap outcomes:** Oversupply → attrition, leave of absence, job sharing, reduced hours; Shortage → full/part-time hiring, contractors, overtime, retention strategies

**Recruitment:**
- **Recruitment:** Locating and encouraging people to apply for jobs
- **Employment branding:** Organization's reputation as an employer

| | **Internal Recruitment** | **External Recruitment** |
|---|---|---|
| **Advantages** | Capitalize on previous investments; reward employees | Bring in new ideas; acquire specialized skills |
| **Disadvantages** | Inbreeding of ideas and attitudes | Legislated equity plan may constrain candidate pool |

**Selection:**
- **Selection:** Choosing from applicant pool those best able to fulfill job requirements
- **Reliability:** Consistency and comparability of outcomes over time
- **Validity:** How well a procedure measures what it intends to measure
- **Sources of applicant information:** Application forms (employer-created, quick); résumés (candidate-created); interviews; references (subjective, not highly predictive)
- **Interview types:**
  - *Structured:* Established answers that can be rated consistently
  - *Unstructured:* Open-ended questions
  - *Structured question types:* **BDI (Behavioural Description Interview)** = past-oriented ("what did you do in X situation?"); **Situational** = hypothetical/future-oriented ("how would you respond to X?"); **Stress-producing** = rapid questioning to assess stress handling
- **Employment assessments:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Cognitive ability test | Brain-based skills: memory, problem-solving, numerical (e.g., GMAT) |
| Personality & interest inventories | Measures personality traits |
| Emotional intelligence & social competence | Personal qualities distinct from cognitive ability |
| Physical ability test | Required for physically demanding jobs (e.g., police, warehouse); valid measure |
| Job sample tests | Perform tasks that are part of the actual work; considered valid |
| Substance abuse tests | Some jobs require; candidate must be informed it may be conditional |

> **Exam tip:** BDI = **past** behaviour ("tell me about a time…"); Situational = **future/hypothetical** ("what would you do if…"). These are the most tested interview distinction.

> **Exam tip:** Reliability = *consistency*; Validity = *accuracy* (measures what it claims to). A test can be reliable but not valid; can't be valid without being reliable.

> **Exam tip:** Trend analysis and management forecasts = **demand** tools. Staffing table, Markov analysis, skills inventory = **supply** tools.

### Ch12 — Onboarding, Training & Development

**Key distinctions:**

| | **Onboarding** | **Training** | **Development** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time frame** | At time of hiring | Short term or immediate | Mid to long term |
| **Focus** | Understanding org, job, work unit | Skill development for current job | Future roles, responsibilities, competency development |
| **Participants** | New hires | Individuals new to roles or underperforming | High performers with future role potential |
| **Goal** | Familiarize & socialize to work culture | Enhance skills in current job | Optimize potential for future |
| **Methods** | Tours, onboarding materials, introductions | Programmed instruction, role-plays, simulation | Coaching, mentoring, conferences, job rotations |

- **Onboarding includes:** Orientation + Training + Development + Socialization
- **Socialization:** Continuing process of integrating org values, norms, beliefs
- **Benefits of onboarding:** Lower turnover, increased productivity/motivation, improved morale, lower training costs, facilitation of learning, reduction of anxiety
- **Knowledge management:** Capturing, distributing, and effectively using knowledge
- **HRD (Human Resource Development):** Integrates onboarding to improve individual, team, and org effectiveness

**ADDIE / Instructional Systems Design (ISD):** — see [[entities/addie-model]]
1. **Assess needs** — at organizational level (strategy), task level (competencies), person level (who needs training); 4 questions: importance? what KSAs needed? what KSAs do employees have? what is the gap?
2. **Design** — training structure, methods, assessment tools, learning principles (clear outcomes, relevance, activity, feedback)
3. **Develop** — create materials; pilot test; review by SMEs
4. **Implement** — deliver training; activate motivation; use observation/demos; positive reinforcement; spaced practice; build on experience; metacognition & self-regulation
5. **Evaluate** — use Kirkpatrick's 4 levels (see [[entities/kirkpatrick-model]])

**Delivery methods:** Classroom instruction, on-the-job training (hands-on with manager/trainer), apprenticeship, co-operative/internship programs, job rotation, self-directed/digital learning, simulation, seminars & conferences  
**Learning methods:** Coaching, role-playing, games, case studies, special projects

**Career Development:**
- **Employee development:** Preparing employee for greater responsibilities/authority in future leadership
- **Talent management:** Preparing a pool of developed employees for future challenges
- **Steps to create Employee Development Plan:** (1) Assess employee needs → (2) Link competencies to business goals → (3) Identify learning activities → (4) Determine resources → (5) Identify barriers
- **Developmental strategies:** Cognitive, behavioural, environmental

**Transfer of training:** Applying learned skills/behaviours on the job to enhance performance (= Kirkpatrick Level 3 – Behaviour)

### Ch13 — Performance Management

**Purposes of PM:**
- **Compensating:** Salary increases, bonuses, pay-for-performance
- **Planning:** Succession planning, identify KSAs, competency gaps
- **Documenting:** Paper trail for HRM decisions; evidence for layoffs
- **Coaching & Developing:** Ongoing coaching, training programs, growth plans, promotion decisions

**Steps in effective PM system:** Clarify work → set goals/plan → provide frequent coaching → formal review → recognize/reward → action plan → identify reasons for ineffective performance

**Why PM systems fail (17 reasons — key exam items):**
1. Not well defined / doesn't encourage outcomes
2. Objectives not prioritized
3. Not connected to company strategy
4. Employees not involved in setting goals → system feels unfair
5. Focus on blame rather than helping
6. Focus on annual review (not ongoing/integrated)
7. Managers dislike face-to-face feedback discussions
8. Managers insufficiently trained → rating errors

**Sources of performance information:**
- Direct (rater sees performance) vs. Indirect (second-hand observations)
- Objective (verifiable/quantitative) vs. Subjective (opinion-based)
- Sources: Manager review, self-review, subordinate review (developmental not admin), peer review, customer review
- **360-degree review:** Combines all sources for well-rounded assessment of both development and administrative purposes

**Performance Review Methods (Non-comparative):**

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Trait — Graphic rating scale** | Rate employee on characteristics (enthusiasm, creativity) on a numerical scale |
| **Behavioural checklist** | Check specific behaviours that should/should not be displayed |
| **BARS (Behaviourally Anchored Rating Scale)** | Series of scales for each performance dimension within a job |
| **MBO / Results method** | Employee & manager agree on goals; performance assessed against results |
| **Balanced scorecard** | Measurement framework translating strategic goals into operational objectives (4 dimensions: financial, customer, internal processes, learning/growth) |

**Comparative methods:**
- **Ranking:** Employees ranked best to worst (subject to halo and recency effects)
- **Forced distribution:** Employees sorted into categories; set proportion must be in each

> **Exam tip:** BARS = scales per performance *dimension*. MBO = manager and employee agree on *individual goals*. Graphic rating scale = rates *traits*. Balanced scorecard ≠ a performance review of individual employees — it translates *org-level* strategy.

> **Exam tip:** Subordinate review is more appropriate for *developmental* than *administrative* purposes — this distinction is commonly tested.

### Ch5/6 — Organizational Culture & Change

**Organizational Culture:**
- **Definition:** Collective understanding of beliefs and values guiding how employees act and behave; includes values (what org stands for) and assumptions (taken-for-granted ways of operating)
- Culture most noticeable when compared to others or when threatened/changed
- Founder and socialization process play large roles

**7 Culture Dimensions (O'Reilly, Chatman & Caldwell):**

| Dimension | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Innovation | Experimenting, opportunity-seeking, risk-taking, few rules, low cautiousness |
| Stability | Predictability, security, rule-oriented |
| Respect for people | Fairness, tolerance |
| Outcome orientation | Action-oriented, high expectations, results-oriented |
| Attention to detail | Precise, analytic |
| Team orientation | Collaboration, people-oriented |
| Aggressiveness | Competitive, low emphasis on social responsibility |

- **Subcultures:** Smaller cultures within the org based on department, training, or occupation
- **Strong culture:** Intense and pervasive values/assumptions; clear rules, no ambiguity
- **Culture effectiveness:** Positively related if aligned with external environment, moderately strong (not cult-like), adaptive and learning-oriented

**Diagnosing Culture:**
- **Symbols (artifacts):** Tangible/intangible non-verbal elements depicting values (e.g., flags, logos, physical spaces)
- **Stories:** Spoken narratives passed between employees humanizing the org, depicting what is accepted
- **Rituals:** Recurring ceremonies/events (e.g., birthday gifts, company picnics)

**Merging Cultures:**
- **Bicultural audit:** Diagnosing cultural relations between companies to determine potential clash

| Strategy | Description | Works best when |
|---|---|---|
| Assimilation | Acquired company adopts acquiring firm's culture | Acquired firm has weak culture |
| Deculturation | Acquiring firm imposes culture on unwilling acquired firm | Acquired firm's culture is dysfunctional |
| Integration | Combines cultures into new composite | Both cultures are weak/have overlapping values |
| Separation | Firms remain distinct entities | Firms operate in different businesses |

**Organizational Socialization:** Process by which employees learn values, norms, beliefs, expected behaviours to assume their roles

**Organizational Change:**
- **Force Field Analysis:** See [[entities/force-field-analysis]] — driving vs. restraining forces; unfreezing → change → refreezing
- **Resistance to change:** Negative valence toward change, fear of the unknown, strong culture
- **Strategies to minimize resistance:** Communication, learning, employee involvement, stress management, negotiation, coercion (last resort — leads to long-term antagonism)
- **Action Research Approach:** Combines research (data collection) + action (change implementation) in cyclical participative process
  1. Diagnosis (problem identification)
  2. Planning action
  3. Taking action (implementation)
  4. Evaluating (fact-finding)
  5. Reflecting and learning
- **Appreciative Inquiry:** Positive, strengths-based approach focusing on what works well; 5 principles: positive, constructionist, simultaneity, poetic, anticipatory
- **Large Group Intervention (LGI):** Engages a significant portion of the org (hundreds or thousands) to shape its future; creates shared understanding, commitment, energy

> **Exam tip:** Bicultural audit = diagnosing cultural *clash*. Organizational socialization = employees learning the culture. These are commonly confused.

> **Exam tip:** Force Field Analysis = Lewin's *unfreezing/refreezing* model. Action Research = cyclical research + action. Appreciative Inquiry = strengths-focused. LGI = mass participation. All four are distinct change approaches.

> **Exam tip:** "Strong culture" question: a very strong culture can *resist* change — this is a risk, not always beneficial.
