# Week 2: Job Analysis & Designing the Work

## Learning Objectives
1. Describe the uses of job analysis for HR managers
2. Discuss steps in conducting job analysis and data collection methods
3. Describe contents of a job description and specification
4. Discuss approaches to setting performance standards
5. Define competencies and competency models
6. Outline key considerations in job design

## Key Content

### Job Analysis vs. Job Design
| | Job Analysis | Job Design |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | What the job *is* now (objective picture) | What the job *should be* (ideal, subjective) |
| Owner | Primarily HR department | Primarily line manager |
| Output | Job description | Redesigned work structure |

### Job Analysis
**Definition:** The process of systematically obtaining information about jobs — tasks & responsibilities, working conditions, competencies needed, and performance standards.

**Three Phases:**
1. **Preparation** — Familiarize with org, determine uses, identify jobs to analyze
2. **Collection** — Sources (human: employees, supervisors, SMEs; nonhuman), data required (job ID, duties, responsibilities, human characteristics, working conditions, performance standards), methods (questionnaires, interviews, focus groups, observation, logs, diaries)
3. **Use** — Job descriptions, job specifications, job standards, competency models

### Task Analysis Approach (4 Steps)
1. Collect task statements (action verb + what + with what + for what)
2. Identify key tasks (rate importance × frequency, set cut-off)
3. Establish task dimensions (3–5 logical categories)
4. Determine competencies linked to each dimension

### KSAOs
| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Knowledge** | Facts/principles/procedures that apply to performance | Labour law, MS Office |
| **Skills** | Learned through experience/training | Financial statements, conflict resolution |
| **Abilities & Others** | General dispositions, personality | Teamwork, empathy, hand-eye coordination |

### Job Description Contents
Job identity (title, location, NOC code, reports-to) · Job summary · Job duties · Working conditions · Approved By · Job specifications (KSAOs) · Performance standards · Date

**NOC:** National Occupation Classification — Canadian reference system categorizing occupations by training, education, experience, and responsibilities.

**Competency vs. KSAO:** Competencies are broader (e.g., "communication" encompasses many KSAOs). A **Competency Model** describes a group of competencies required for a job. A **Competency Matrix** shows competency levels required across multiple jobs.

### Job Description Do's & Don'ts
| Common Problems | Best Practices |
|---|---|
| Vague terms, not updated | Concise, direct, action verbs |
| Specs not related to job (discrimination risk) | Match actual bona fide requirements |
| Omits important tasks | Include actual duties |
| Limits scope, no performance standards | Include standards; not too restrictive |

> **Exam tip:** Including irrelevant specifications (e.g., "10 years experience" for a 2-year job) risks discrimination.

### Job Design
**Definition:** The process of defining and organizing tasks, roles, and other processes.

**Employee Considerations — Emotional Labour:**
- **Emotions:** Psychophysiological episodes that create readiness; can trigger facial expressions
- **Emotional Labour (Hochschild, 1983):** Regulating emotional expressions as part of professional work
- **Emotional Regulation:** Managing/responding to emotional experiences appropriately
- Jobs with high emotional labour: healthcare, emergency services, teaching, customer service, social work

**Strategies for Emotional Regulation:**
- *Cognitive:* shifting attention, reframing situation, suppressing/amplifying emotions
- *Behavioural:* modifying current situation, exiting/entering a situation, physical activity

### Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham)
See [[entities/job-characteristics-model]] for full detail.

Core characteristics → Psychological states → Outcomes:
- **Variety + Identity + Significance** → Meaningfulness
- **Autonomy** → Responsibility
- **Feedback** → Knowledge of results
→ ↑ Motivation, Performance, Satisfaction; ↓ Absenteeism, Turnover

**Work Design Model (Humphrey, Nahrang & Morgeson, 2007):** Adds social characteristics (interdependence, feedback from others, social support) and work context (physical demands, ergonomics).

### Job Design Strategies
- **Job rotation:** Move employees between jobs at intervals (↑ variety)
- **Job enlargement:** Add more types of tasks (horizontal expansion)
- **Job enrichment:** Add higher-order responsibilities (vertical expansion)
- **Employee involvement:** Employee initiates/shapes own work
- **Work teams:** Work organized around teams and processes

## Exercises & Cases
- Mini Job Analysis activity (select 2 work tasks, rate importance/frequency, list KSAOs)
- Work Design Questionnaire (Morgeson & Humphreys, 2006) — identify high/low characteristics
- Group discussion: strategies to improve 2 characteristics; implementation planning

## Exam-Relevant Highlights
- Job analysis vs. job design distinction (objective vs. ideal)
- Task analysis 4 steps — especially the cut-off for key tasks
- KSAOs: knowledge vs. skill vs. ability is frequently tested with examples
- JCM: which characteristic creates which psychological state (autonomy → responsibility is frequently tested)
- Emotional labour is often overlooked but tested
- Job enlargement (horizontal) vs. job enrichment (vertical) is a classic distinction
