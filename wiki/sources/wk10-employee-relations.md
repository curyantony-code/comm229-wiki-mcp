## Organizational Culture

### Definition
Collective understanding of beliefs and values that guide how employees act and behave. The "personality of a company." Normally established by founders and enforced by middle management. Socialization starts from Day 1.

### Espoused vs. Enacted Values
- **Espoused values:** Values the company *wants* others to believe guide its decisions
- **Enacted values:** Values that *actually* guide and influence decisions and behaviour
> **Instructor note:** Organizational culture is defined by **enacted** (not espoused) values.

### Artifacts (4 Types)
Observable symbols and signs of culture — needed because culture is usually ambiguous and fragmented:
1. **Stories & legends** — told across generations of employees (founder stories, "can the little person rise?")
2. **Language** — unique terms, phrases, abbreviations
3. **Rituals** — recurring behaviours (e.g., Ben & Jerry's Free Cone Day)
4. **Ceremonies** — formal events
5. **Physical structures & symbols** — logos, mottos, office layout

> **Exam tip:** Culture is ambiguous and fragmented — you need multiple artifacts to accurately decipher it.

### Organizational Subcultures
- **Dominant culture:** Values/assumptions shared most widely
- **Subcultures:** Located throughout the organization
- **Countercultures:** Oppose aspects of the dominant culture; serve as surveillance/critical review function and source of emerging values

## Culture Strength

### Definition
How widely and deeply employees hold the company's dominant values and assumptions. Most employees understand and embrace the culture. Long-lasting — often originating with founders.

### Three Functions of Strong Cultures
1. **Control system** — guides behaviour without explicit rules
2. **Social glue** — creates cohesion and belonging
3. **Sense-making** — helps employees interpret what happens around them

### Three Contingencies (When Culture Strength Helps)
1. Culture content must be **aligned with the environment**
2. Strength must not cross into **cult-like** pressure
3. Culture must include **adaptive culture** (willingness to change)

> **Exam tip:** Strong culture is NOT always beneficial. It can suppress creativity, limit diversity, and entrench unethical norms.

### Attraction-Selection-Attrition (ASA) Theory
Culture strength increases through:
- **Attraction:** Applicants self-select based on compatible values
- **Selection:** Firms select applicants with compatible values
- **Attrition:** Employees with incompatible values quit or are removed

### Changing Organizational Culture
1. Leaders modelling desired culture
2. Aligning artifacts with desired culture
3. Aligning rewards and recognition with desired culture
4. Aligning workforce management practices with desired culture (attrition, fit)

## Organizational Socialization

### Definition
The process by which individuals learn the values, expected behaviours, and social knowledge necessary to assume their roles in the organization.

**Learning process:** Organizational comprehension, culture, performance expectations, power dynamics, social relationships
**Adjustment process:** Develop new work roles, social identity, team norms

### Psychological Contracts
Individual beliefs about the terms and conditions of a reciprocal exchange agreement.
| Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Transactional** | Short-term economic exchanges; well-defined responsibilities |
| **Relational** | Long-term attachments; broad range of mutual obligations |

### Stages of Socialization
1. Pre-arrival (before joining)
2. Encounter (first weeks)
3. Change and acquisition (adjusting and fitting in)

### Improving Socialization
- **RJP (Realistic Job Preview):** Balance of positive *and* negative information — sets accurate expectations, reduces turnover
- **Socialization agents:**
  - *Supervisors:* Technical information, performance feedback, job duties
  - *Co-workers:* Ideal when accessible, role model, tolerant, supportive

## Attitudes

### Definition
A fairly stable evaluative tendency towards an object. Consists of:
1. **Beliefs:** What you perceive to be true
2. **Feelings:** Positive or negative evaluations
3. **Behavioural propensity:** Tendency to engage in certain behaviours

**Attitude formation:** Perceived environment + emotion episodes → attitudes → behaviour

## Job Satisfaction

### Definition
The degree to which you like/dislike your job.

**Global job satisfaction:** "How satisfied are you overall?"
**Facet job satisfaction:** Tasks, coworkers, supervisor, pay, career opportunities

### EVLN Model — Responses to Dissatisfaction
| Response | Description |
|---|---|
| **Exit** | Leave organization, change department, change supervisor |
| **Voice** | Seek change rather than withdrawing |
| **Loyalty** | Wait for the situation to change |
| **Neglect** | Reduce effort; increase counterproductive behaviour and withdrawal |

### Consequences of Job Satisfaction
Turnover · Absenteeism · Organizational Citizenship Behaviour (OCB) · Customer satisfaction and profitability · Job performance

### Determinants of Job Satisfaction
Dispositions · Emotions & moods · Job characteristics · Fairness · Goals

> **Exam tip:** The relationship between satisfaction and performance is bidirectional (chicken-and-egg). Satisfied employees tend to have *somewhat* higher performance, but this is not absolute.

### Measuring Job Satisfaction
Two approaches: global rating ("Pleasant/Bad") or facet-specific scale (JDI — work, pay, promotion, supervision, coworkers).

## Organizational Commitment

### Three Types
| Type | Definition | Drive | Consequences |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Affective** | Emotional attachment and identification | Stay because they *want to* | ↑ Motivation, OCB, performance, loyalty |
| **Continuance** | Calculative — too much to lose | Stay because they *have to* | ↓ Performance, ↓ OCB, more grievances |
| **Normative** | Moral duty and obligation | Stay because they *should* | Moderate positive outcomes |

> **Exam tip:** Affective commitment is the *most desirable*. Continuance commitment can coexist with poor performance.

### Building Affective Commitment
- **Justice and support:** Organizational justice and employee well-being
- **Trust:** Positive expectations in risk situations; management trusting employees
- **Shared values:** Value congruence between employee and firm
- **Employee involvement:** Psychological ownership and social identity
- **Organizational comprehension:** Clear mental model of the organization enables identification

## Employee Attitude Survey
Systematic method of determining what employees think about their organization.
- Topics: working conditions, supervision, HR policies, etc.
- HR should provide summary of results and take concrete improvement actions
